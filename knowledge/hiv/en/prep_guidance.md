# Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)

Source notes: Adapted from WHO and CDC PrEP guidelines. Localized for Tanzania. This is supportive information.

## What is PrEP?

PrEP is a daily medication taken BEFORE potential HIV exposure to prevent HIV infection. When taken consistently, PrEP reduces the risk of getting HIV from sex by about 99%.

## Who Should Consider PrEP?

PrEP is for HIV-negative people who have an ongoing risk of HIV, such as:
- People with an HIV-positive partner
- People who do not consistently use condoms
- People with multiple sexual partners
- People who have had a recent STI
- People who inject drugs and share equipment
- Sex workers
- Anyone who feels they may be at risk

## How PrEP Works

- PrEP contains antiretroviral medication (usually TDF/FTC or TAF/FTC)
- You take one pill daily
- It takes about 7 days of daily use to reach full protection for receptive anal sex
- It takes about 21 days for full protection for receptive vaginal sex and injection drug use
- PrEP must be taken consistently to be effective

## Getting PrEP

- Visit a healthcare provider or CTC for an HIV test and PrEP assessment
- Through Bepawaa: Book a PrEP consultation or find PrEP-providing facilities
- PrEP requires a negative HIV test before starting
- Regular follow-up visits every 3 months for HIV testing and prescription refills

## Side Effects

- Most people have no side effects
- Some may experience: mild nausea, headache, or stomach discomfort in the first few weeks
- These usually go away within a few days to weeks
- Serious side effects are rare — talk to your provider if concerned

## PrEP Myths vs Facts

- Myth: PrEP is only for certain groups → Fact: PrEP is for anyone at risk of HIV
- Myth: Taking PrEP means you have HIV → Fact: PrEP is prevention for HIV-negative people
- Myth: PrEP replaces condoms → Fact: PrEP prevents HIV but not other STIs; condoms add extra protection
- Myth: PrEP is too expensive → Fact: PrEP is available free or at low cost at many facilities in Tanzania

## Stopping PrEP

- You can stop PrEP if your risk changes
- Talk to your provider before stopping
- Continue taking PrEP for 28 days after last possible exposure
- Get tested for HIV when stopping and 3 months later
